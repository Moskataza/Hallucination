Hallucination definition:
A hallucination occurs when the model's reasoning or final answer contains information that is not faithfully supported by the image or by valid logical inference grounded in the image.

Important principles:
- An incorrect answer alone does not necessarily imply hallucination.
- A correct answer can still contain hallucination if the reasoning or visual evidence is unsupported.
- Judge only from the image, question, reference answer, model reasoning, and model final answer.

Taxonomy:

Factual Hallucination:
- OBJ: Object/Category hallucination. Claims the presence, absence, or category of an object not supported by the image.
- ATT: Attribute hallucination. Misstates color, shape, size, count, material, OCR text, chart value, or other visual attributes.
- SPA: Spatial/Relational hallucination. Misstates relative position, direction, containment, adjacency, or other spatial relations.

Logical Hallucination:
- IR: Invalid reasoning. Uses incorrect premises, unsupported assumptions, or invalid inference steps.
- CI: Unjustified causality. Infers causality, intent, or functionality without visual evidence.
- INC: Internal inconsistency. The reasoning conflicts with the final answer or contradicts itself.
- SO: Semantic over-attribution. Assigns semantic roles, functions, or meanings that cannot be determined from visual evidence.
